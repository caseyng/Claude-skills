## 1. The Philosophy

This framework is built on three disciplines working together:

**Structural dependency** — find companies that are load-bearing walls, not decorative ones. Load-bearing walls don't get removed. They generate durable revenue, attract serious acquirers, and survive cycles that destroy speculative names.

**Value discipline** — never pay for perfection. Pay for a good business at a fair-to-cheap price with a margin of safety that protects you if you're wrong on timing.

**Patient entry** — the market comes to you. You do not chase it. A position you don't own yet costs you nothing. A position entered at the wrong price costs you both money and confidence.

These three are not separate strategies. They are one strategy applied in sequence.

## 2. How to Surface Stocks

### Step 1 — Ask the Structural Question First

Don't start with financial metrics. Start here:

- What companies sit inside a supply chain that cannot easily replace them?
- What businesses hold certifications, licenses, or processes that take years to replicate?
- What companies, if bankrupt, would cause a competitor or customer real operational pain?
- What sectors have high switching costs — where changing vendors means retooling, recertifying, or rebuilding?

### Step 2 — Find the Company One Level Below the Famous One

The best opportunities are rarely the headline names. Look beneath:

| Famous Company | The Hidden Dependency |
|---|---|
| Lam Research | Ichor Holdings — supplies their fluid delivery systems |
| Singapore Airlines | SIA Engineering — maintains their aircraft |
| Every chip fab on earth | Photronics — makes the photomasks without which no chip can be made |
| Offshore energy operators | Seatrium — one of few yards globally that can build complex offshore structures |

**The rule:** find the company the famous company cannot function without.

### Step 3 — Identify the Category

Structural dependency clusters in specific sectors:

| Category | Why It Qualifies |
|---|---|
| Semiconductor infrastructure | Photomasks, fluid delivery, specialty coatings — the invisible layer beneath chip manufacturing |
| MRO and certification | Aircraft maintenance, defense systems — replicating certifications takes years |
| Energy and utilities | Power generation, water treatment — national-level dependencies |
| Offshore and marine engineering | Complex fabrication for oil, gas, renewables — only a handful of yards globally can execute |
| Defense supply chain | Components and systems locked to sovereign contracts |

## 3. The Metrics

These are the benchmarks that matter — drawn directly from what Buffett, Lynch, Greenblatt, and Marks actually use. Not all metrics apply to every sector. Weight them by context.

---

### 3.1 ROIC — Return on Invested Capital
**Source: Greenblatt (primary), Buffett**

`ROIC = Net Operating Profit After Tax ÷ Invested Capital`

The single most important number. It tells you whether the moat is actually generating returns or just sounding good.

| ROIC | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Above 20% | Exceptional. The moat is real and working. |
| 15–20% | Excellent. Worth paying a fair price. |
| 10–15% | Good. Acceptable with margin of safety. |
| 8–10% | Marginal. Needs a significant price discount. |
| Below 8% | Value destruction. Walk away regardless of the narrative. |

**Critical refinement:** benchmark ROIC against the sector median, not just the absolute number. A 12% ROIC in capital-intensive shipbuilding (Seatrium) is exceptional. A 12% ROIC for an IP-heavy software company is mediocre. The company should clear its sector median by at least 20%.

**The Nanofilm lesson:** proprietary nanotechnology, real customers, genuine process complexity — and 1.6% ROIC. A leaking moat is worse than no moat. It costs more to maintain and fools you longer.

---

### 3.2 ROE — Return on Equity
**Source: Buffett**

`ROE = Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity`

Buffett's preferred measure of management quality. It shows how efficiently the company uses shareholder money to generate profit.

| ROE | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Above 15% consistently | Strong management. Capital is being deployed well. |
| 10–15% | Acceptable. Watch trend direction. |
| Below 10% | Poor capital allocation. Question the management team. |

**The consistency test matters more than any single year.** Buffett looks for ROE above 15% for at least 5 consecutive years. One good year proves nothing. Five consecutive years proves a system.

**Watch for the debt trick:** a company can inflate ROE by taking on debt. Always cross-check ROE against the debt-to-equity ratio. High ROE + high debt = manufactured appearance of quality.

---

### 3.3 Free Cash Flow Yield
**Source: Buffett ("owner earnings"), Greenblatt (earnings yield)**

`FCF Yield = Free Cash Flow per Share ÷ Share Price`

This is the real earnings yield — what the business actually generates in cash after maintaining and growing itself, expressed as a percentage of what you're paying for it.

| FCF Yield | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Above 8% | Genuinely cheap. Strong value signal. |
| 5–8% | Reasonable. Acceptable with quality moat. |
| 3–5% | Priced for perfection. Requires exceptional quality to justify. |
| Below 3% | Expensive. The market has priced in significant future growth. |

**Why FCF over earnings:** reported earnings can be manipulated through accounting choices. Free cash flow is harder to fake. Buffett calls it "owner earnings" — the cash a business actually generates for its owners after all necessary reinvestment.

**Cross-check against analyst targets:** if the analyst consensus implies 25% upside but FCF yield is below 3%, the target is built on growth assumptions, not current value. That's a different — and riskier — bet.

---

### 3.4 Debt-to-Equity Ratio
**Source: Buffett, Graham**

`D/E = Total Liabilities ÷ Shareholders' Equity`

Measures financial risk. A company with too much debt has less room to survive a downturn, less flexibility to invest in growth, and is more vulnerable to interest rate changes.

| D/E | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Below 0.5 | Conservative. Strong balance sheet. |
| 0.5–1.0 | Moderate. Acceptable for stable businesses. |
| 1.0–2.0 | High. Requires strong and predictable cash flows to justify. |
| Above 2.0 | Dangerous. One bad quarter can create a crisis. |

**Sector context applies:** capital-intensive industries (shipbuilding, utilities, REITs) carry structurally higher debt. Benchmark against sector peers, not an absolute number.

**The Buffett rule:** a company should be able to repay all its debt from earnings within 3–5 years under normal conditions. If it can't, the debt is a structural risk, not a financing tool.

---

### 3.5 Earnings Consistency — The Predictability Test
**Source: Buffett, Lynch**

Neither Buffett nor Lynch trust a single year of good numbers. They want to see a track record.

Run this check:
- Have earnings grown in at least 7 of the last 10 years?
- Has the company been profitable in every year of the last 5?
- Has revenue grown consistently — or does it spike and collapse?

Consistent, predictable earnings compound. Erratic earnings — even with impressive highs — introduce timing risk that destroys the patient investor's edge.

**Lynch's specific test:** he looked for companies growing earnings at 15–20% annually in a sustainable, non-cyclical way. Faster than that is often unsustainable. Slower than 10% in a structural dependency play suggests the moat isn't growing.

---

### 3.6 PEG Ratio — Growth-Adjusted Valuation
**Source: Lynch**

`PEG = P/E Ratio ÷ Annual Earnings Growth Rate`

Lynch's signature metric. It corrects for the problem that a high P/E can be cheap if growth is fast, and a low P/E can be expensive if growth is zero.

| PEG | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Below 1.0 | Undervalued relative to growth. Lynch's buy zone. |
| 1.0 | Fairly valued. Growth is exactly priced in. |
| Above 1.5 | Expensive relative to growth. Requires exceptional quality. |
| Above 2.0 | The market is pricing in optimism. Walk away. |

**Limitation:** PEG breaks down for companies with negative earnings or near-zero growth. Use FCF yield for those instead.

---

### 3.7 Margin of Safety
**Source: Graham (founding concept), Buffett**

The margin of safety is the gap between intrinsic value and the price you pay. It is your protection against being wrong — about the business, the timing, or the market.

`Margin of Safety = (Intrinsic Value - Current Price) ÷ Intrinsic Value`

| Margin of Safety | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Above 30% | Strong protection. High conviction entry zone. |
| 20–30% | Solid. Acceptable for high-quality structural plays. |
| 10–20% | Thin. Requires exceptional business quality to justify. |
| Below 10% | Insufficient. One bad earnings quarter eliminates your buffer. |

**In practice for this framework:** your entry limit order IS your margin of safety mechanism. Setting a limit 3–5% below current price on a stock trading 20–30% below analyst consensus gives you a combined margin of safety of 23–35%. That's the zone.

---

### 3.8 Market Cycle Position
**Source: Marks**

Marks adds one dimension no other investor focuses on as systematically: where are we in the cycle?

Every market moves through expansion, peak, contraction, and trough. Most investors buy at the peak because that's when confidence is highest. Marks trains himself to do the opposite.

Ask these questions before entering any position:
- Is the broader market index near multi-year highs? (Caution)
- Is credit cheap and widely available? (Late cycle — caution)
- Is the sector you're entering coming off a 40%+ run? (Wait)
- Is there fear in the news cycle about this sector specifically? (Opportunity)

**Applied to this framework:** this is why near-52-week-high stocks go on the watch list, not the buy list. Price tells you something about cycle position. Respect it.

## 4. The Stress Test

Every candidate runs through this before it earns a place on the watch list. More than two failures = remove it.

### The Six Tests

| # | Test | Pass Condition | Fail Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Structural Dependency | Customer cannot replace them within 36 months | Replacement possible within 12 months |
| 2 | ROIC vs Sector Median | ROIC exceeds sector median by 20%+ | ROIC below 8% absolute, or below sector median |
| 3 | Customer Concentration | No single customer above 40% of revenue | One customer above 50% — dependency is inverted |
| 4 | Balance Sheet | D/E below sector median, debt repayable within 5 years from earnings | Debt level requires constant refinancing or equity raises |
| 5 | Earnings Consistency | Profitable 5 consecutive years, growing 7 of last 10 | Earnings erratic, cyclical, or declining trend |
| 6 | Governance | No fraud, no serial dilution, management incentives aligned with shareholders | Prior corruption, excessive executive pay vs revenue, repeated equity dilution |

### Red Flags That Override the Thesis

These are automatic disqualifiers regardless of how good the story sounds:

- Customer concentration above 50% — the moat runs to them, not from you
- ROIC below 5% on a proprietary technology business
- Stock at 52-week high with analyst upside below 15%
- Debt that cannot be repaid within 5 years from current earnings
- Two or more governance incidents in the last decade
- Management selling significant personal shareholdings while recommending the stock publicly

### The Switching Cost Timeline Test (Dependency Scoring)

| Replacement Timeline | Score | Dependency Level |
|---|---|---|
| Under 12 months | 0 | Weak — fails the test |
| 12–24 months | 1 | Marginal — only pass with exceptional other metrics |
| 24–36 months | 2 | Moderate — acceptable |
| 36–60 months | 3 | Strong — genuine structural dependency |
| Over 60 months or certification-locked | 4 | Exceptional — load-bearing wall |

Require a score of 2 or above to proceed.

## 5. Entry Price Discipline

### The Three Inputs

1. **Analyst consensus target** — the ceiling. Cross-check against FCF yield to verify it's intrinsic, not optimistic.
2. **52-week range position** — lower half of range = potential value. Upper half = wait.
3. **ATR-adjusted discount** — set limit at least 1.5x the average daily trading range below current price. This ensures you're buying on a genuine down day, not normal noise.

### The Six Entry Rules

1. Analyst upside must be 15% or more from today's price. Below 15% — skip.
2. Stock must be in the lower half of its 52-week range. Near highs — watch only.
3. Set limit order at ATR-adjusted discount. Never market orders outside RTH.
4. Use RTH (Regular Trading Hours) session only. Pre/post spreads punish small positions.
5. Re-place the order daily if it expires unfilled. The order working is not the same as the thesis working.
6. Fund entirely from cash. Margin on a patient, dip-buying strategy is the wrong tool — it punishes patience and creates forced-sell risk at exactly the wrong moment.

### ATR Quick Guide

Before setting your limit, check the stock's Average True Range (ATR) — available on any charting tool. Multiply by 1.5. That is your minimum discount from current price.

*Example: PLAB ATR = $0.80. Minimum limit discount = $1.20. Current price $34.10 → limit at $32.90 or below.*

## 6. Position Sizing

Size to conviction, not to excitement.

| Conviction Level | Description | Position Size |
|---|---|---|
| High | Load-bearing wall, clean balance sheet, near range lows, strong ROIC | S$3,000–5,000 / US$2,000–4,000 |
| Medium | Good structural dependency, moderate metrics, middle of range | S$2,000–3,000 / US$1,500–2,500 |
| Low / Testing | Thesis developing, some uncertainty remaining | S$1,000–1,500 / US$800–1,200 |
| Speculative | Asymmetric bet — BrainChip type | Maximum 2–3% of total portfolio |

### Portfolio Concentration Rules

- Maximum 40% in any single currency
- Maximum 35% in any single sector
- No more than 2 positions in the same regional market at the same time
- Never more than 10% of total portfolio in speculative positions

## 7. After You Enter — Monitoring and Exit

### Write the Pre-Mortem First

Before placing any order, write three sentences:

> *Why I am buying:* [specific structural reason]
> *What would prove me wrong:* [one specific, observable, measurable condition]
> *My exit trigger:* [a price level, an event, or a metric threshold]

This is not optional. It is the discipline that separates a conviction hold from a panic sell when the price drops 15% on a bad day.

The prove me wrong condition is a living signal. When material news touches a competitor or the structural basis of the moat — reopen the pre-mortem before checking the price. Price is the last thing you look at. Not the first.

---

### What to Monitor

- Earnings dates — results within 20% of consensus keep the thesis intact
- Customer concentration changes — anchor customer shifts require immediate re-evaluation
- Competitor moves — a competitor acquiring superior technology or certification narrows your moat
- Dilution announcements — any equity raise dilutes your position and signals cash stress
- ROIC trend — if ROIC deteriorates two consecutive quarters, re-examine the moat

**Monitoring rhythm:** not daily (noise). Not buy-and-forget (misses thesis breaks). Earnings cycle is the right frequency. Immediate only if a prove me wrong condition is triggered or material competitor news breaks.

---

### The Exit Framework

Two exit types. They are not the same decision. Conflating them is how you hold a broken thesis because the price hasn't dropped enough to feel like a sell.

#### Rule 1 — Forced Exit
Thesis broken. Sell 100% regardless of price. No exceptions.

Triggered by: the prove me wrong condition in the pre-mortem is met. The reason you bought no longer exists. Whether you are up 40% or down 30% is irrelevant.

#### Rule 2 — Planned Exit (Capital Gains positions)
Stock reaches analyst consensus target. Default action: **sell 50%**. Hold the remainder only if the last two earnings reports show growth above your prove me wrong threshold. If growth is below threshold — full exit, not partial.

**Valuation gap exception — full exit always.** If the position was entered primarily because the market mispriced it — not because of long-term compounding potential — take the full gain at target. The thesis was the gap, not the business trajectory. Once the gap closes, the thesis has delivered. Redeploy or re-enter lower. Don't chase residual upside after the original reason for buying no longer exists.

#### Rule 3 — Planned Exit (Yield positions)
Dividend drops below your threshold. Exit. Do not wait for price to confirm what the dividend already told you. Dividend health is the signal — not price movement.

#### The Meta-Rule
If material news touches a competitor or the structural basis of the moat — reopen the pre-mortem before checking the price. This applies regardless of position type, profit or loss status, or how recent your last review was.

---

### When to Hold

- Short-term price drops on broad market selloffs — noise, not signal
- One bad earnings quarter when structural dependency is unchanged
- Analyst downgrades that don't alter the fundamental thesis

## 8. Adversarial Testing — The Sounding Board Protocol

No framework is foolproof. The purpose of adversarial testing is not to find reasons to avoid investing. It is to surface the specific assumption that, if wrong, breaks the thesis. Find that assumption. Test it explicitly. Then decide.

### The Five Adversarial Questions

Before entering any position, run these in order:

**1. The Inversion Test**
*"What would have to be true for this investment to lose 50% of its value and stay there?"*
Write the scenario. If it's plausible, size the position accordingly.

**2. The Customer Test**
*"If the anchor customer called this company tomorrow and said they were switching, what would happen to revenue?"*
If the answer is "catastrophic," the dependency runs the wrong way.

**3. The Replication Test**
*"How long would it take a well-funded competitor to build what this company has?"*
Under 2 years — the moat is shallow. Over 5 years with certification requirements — it's genuine.

**4. The Management Test**
*"Is management getting rich while shareholders wait?"*
Check: executive compensation as a percentage of revenue. Check: insider selling patterns. Check: history of equity raises that diluted shareholders.

**5. The Cycle Test (Marks)**
*"Am I buying this because the thesis is sound, or because the last 12 months of price action made it feel safe?"*
Momentum feels like conviction. They are not the same thing.

### The Devil's Advocate Session

Before every purchase, take the bear case seriously. Spend 15 minutes constructing the strongest possible argument against the investment. Then ask: does the bull case survive this argument?

If it does, proceed.
If it doesn't, you've saved yourself from a mistake.

This is the function that Charlie Munger served for Buffett for 60 years. You don't have a Munger. This protocol is the substitute.

### Six-Monthly Framework Review

Every six months, take one position that underperformed and run it back through the full framework. Find which test failed to catch the problem. Update the framework accordingly.

The framework is a living document. A framework that never changes is a framework that hasn't been tested.

## 9. Lessons from the Masters

### Warren Buffett — Circle of Competence and Mr. Market

Buffett's most underrated rule is not "buy great businesses." It is the prerequisite: only buy businesses you can fully understand. Inside your circle of competence, you can evaluate quality and judge whether a moat is real. Outside it, you are guessing — and guessing with confidence is the most dangerous state in investing.

His Mr. Market concept is equally important. The market is a manic-depressive who offers you prices daily. You are not obligated to trade with him. You wait until he offers something sensible. Passing on ST Engineering, SATS, and SIA Engineering because they were near highs is Mr. Market thinking in action.

**What to take from him:**
- Narrow your sectors. Two or three, gone deep, beats twenty sectors gone shallow.
- Ask: "Could I explain this business clearly in two minutes?" If not, you don't own it yet.
- Hold cash when nothing qualifies. Discipline to wait is as important as discipline to act.

**Key metric he relies on:** ROE above 15% consistently, D/E below 0.5, earnings predictability over 10 years.

---

### Peter Lynch — The Stalwart and the One-Paragraph Thesis

Lynch ran Fidelity Magellan from 1977 to 1990 and averaged 29.2% annual returns — the best sustained record of any mutual fund manager in history. His edge: he invested in boring, essential, unexciting businesses that institutions ignored because they generated no headlines. He called them stalwarts.

Photronics is a textbook Lynch stalwart. It makes photomasks. Nobody outside the semiconductor industry knows what that is. Every chip fab on earth depends on them. Lynch would have found it by asking a semiconductor engineer what they couldn't live without.

His most transferable discipline: the one-paragraph thesis. If you cannot explain why you own a stock in three sentences, you don't understand it well enough to own it. That clarity is your anchor when prices fall and emotion rises.

**What to take from him:**
- Write the thesis before you buy. Not after.
- Look for earnings growth of 15–20% sustained — not spiky.
- PEG below 1.0 is his buy signal. Above 2.0, walk away.

**Key metric he relies on:** PEG ratio, earnings consistency, revenue growth trend.

---

### Joel Greenblatt — ROIC First, Price Second, Everything Else Noise

Greenblatt's Magic Formula ranks every stock on two things only: return on capital and earnings yield. Buy the top of both lists simultaneously. Hold for a year. Repeat. That's it. He ran Gotham Capital at 40% annualised returns for over a decade with this approach.

The insight: most investors focus on revenue, brand, management charisma, market size. Greenblatt strips all of that away. A company either generates strong returns on its capital or it doesn't. A company is either cheap relative to its earnings or it isn't. Everything else is storytelling.

**What to take from him:**
- Run ROIC before anything else. It is the first filter, not the fifth.
- ROIC must beat sector median by at least 20% to qualify as a genuine moat.
- Use FCF yield to cross-check analyst price targets. Targets not anchored in FCF are wishful thinking.

**Key metric he relies on:** ROIC vs sector median, earnings yield (FCF yield), EV/EBIT.

---

### Howard Marks — Second-Level Thinking, Cycles, and Risk as Permanent Loss

Marks defines risk differently from everyone else. Risk is not volatility. Risk is the probability of permanent loss of capital. A stock dropping 30% temporarily is not risk if the underlying business is intact. Buying at the wrong point in the cycle at the wrong price and being forced to sell at the bottom — that is risk.

His second-level thinking is the adversarial protocol in this framework. First-level thinking: "BrainChip has great technology, I should buy it." Second-level thinking: "BrainChip has great technology, the market knows this, but the market also knows they're burning cash faster than they're earning it — so who exactly is going to be surprised by good news?" The second question is where the edge lives.

**What to take from him:**
- Always ask: what is the consensus view, and what do I know that the consensus is missing?
- Fear in the market is your ally. Comfort is your warning signal.
- Write the pre-mortem before every purchase. Imagining the failure before it happens is the only time you can think clearly about it.

**Key metric he relies on:** market cycle position, sentiment indicators, credit availability as a cycle signal.

---

### The One Gap Across All Four

Every one of them kept written records of their reasoning — before they had skin in the game. The pre-mortem in Section 7 is that practice formalised. Do it every time, without exception.

## 10. Quick Reference Checklist

Run through this before placing any order. Every answer must be yes.

### Surfacing
- [ ] Does this company pass the switching cost timeline test? (Score 2 or above)
- [ ] Is it one level below the famous company in the supply chain?
- [ ] Is the sector one I understand well enough to evaluate?

### Metrics
- [ ] ROIC above 10% absolute and 20% above sector median?
- [ ] ROE above 15% for at least 3 of the last 5 years?
- [ ] FCF yield above 5%?
- [ ] D/E below sector median and debt repayable within 5 years?
- [ ] Earnings consistent — profitable 5 consecutive years?
- [ ] PEG below 1.5?

### Stress Test
- [ ] No single customer above 40% of revenue?
- [ ] No governance incidents in the last 5 years?
- [ ] ROIC trend stable or improving (not deteriorating)?
- [ ] None of the automatic disqualifier red flags present?

### Entry
- [ ] Analyst upside 15% or more from current price?
- [ ] Stock in lower half of 52-week range?
- [ ] Limit set at ATR-adjusted discount — not a market order?
- [ ] Funded entirely from cash, not margin?
- [ ] Session set to RTH only?

### Adversarial
- [ ] Devil's advocate case constructed and bull case survives it?
- [ ] Inversion test run — 50% loss scenario is implausible?
- [ ] Management test passed — no concerning insider selling?
- [ ] Cycle test — buying on thesis, not on recent price momentum?

### Pre-Mortem Written
- [ ] Why I am buying: _______________
- [ ] What would prove me wrong: _______________
- [ ] My exit trigger: _______________

### Exit Rules Confirmed
- [ ] Forced exit condition defined — what breaks the thesis?
- [ ] Planned exit defined — full or 50% at target?
- [ ] Yield positions — dividend threshold set?
- [ ] Meta-rule understood — reopen pre-mortem before checking price on any material news?

---

**If any answer is no — pause. Resolve it before placing the order.**
