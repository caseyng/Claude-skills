# Job Application Approach

A reusable methodology for cover letters and resume tailoring, built from Ng Kok Chuan's profile and job preferences.

---

## What This Is For

Every job application has two documents: the cover letter and the resume. They serve different purposes.

The resume is evidence. It answers: what has this person done, and did it work?

The cover letter is thinking. It answers: does this person understand our problem, and do they see things we have not?

Tailor both. But tailor them differently.

---

## Before Writing Anything

### Read the job description with two passes.

**Pass 1: Surface read.** What are they asking for? Skills, experience, responsibilities.

**Pass 2: Gap read.** What have they not mentioned that they will need? What problems are implied by the role but not named? What will bite them if nobody thinks about it?

The gap read is where the cover letter lives.

### Check the job against the preferences file.

Before investing time, run the role against JobPreferences.md:

- Green lights: proceed.
- Yellow lights: dig deeper before writing. Flag them.
- Red lights: decline or negotiate hard. Do not write a cover letter for a red light role without resolving the blocker first.

### Ask: what is the honest fit?

Do not oversell. Do not undersell. Name the gaps honestly in the letter if they are closeable. Do not claim experience that does not exist.

## Cover Letter Approach

### The goal

Not to summarise the resume. Not to repeat their job description back at them.

The goal is to show you have already started thinking about their problems before you walked in the door.

### Structure

1. **Who you are.** One or two sentences. Specific, not generic. What is the pattern across your career?

2. **What stood out.** Name the things in the job description that you have a genuine and specific perspective on. Not everything. Two or three at most.

3. **What you see that they have not said.** This is the differentiator. One observation per gap. Each paragraph should show you understand their problem at a level they did not articulate in the posting.

4. **The ceiling.** If there is a logical next step the role will generate that they have not considered yet, name it. Show you are thinking beyond day one.

5. **One proof point.** A project, a system, a decision. Something concrete that shows you can do this, not just think about it.

6. **The close.** One sentence. What you want and why this role fits. Do not summarise what you just said.

### Rules

**Do not mirror their words.** If you are using their exact phrases, rewrite. It reads like someone who read the job description carefully, not someone who has done the work.

**Do not explain why something resonates. Just say that it does.**

**No hedging connectors.** No "however", "in addition", "that being said." If the next sentence needs a connector to make sense, rewrite it.

**No dashes.** Use colons, full stops, or restructure the sentence.

**Short paragraphs. One idea each.**

**End when the point is made.**

**Target length: under 500 words.** Every sentence must earn its place.

### The "what you see that they have not said" test

For each observation you want to include, ask:

- Is this actually implied by their job description, or am I projecting?
- Can I connect it to a real experience or project?
- Does naming this make me look like someone who has been here before, or like someone pitching a consulting engagement?

If it reads like a hard sell, reframe it. Show you understand the problem first. The solution follows naturally.

### Tone

Conversational and direct. Not formal, not casual. Confident without being arrogant. Say the uncomfortable thing plainly. Trust the reader to be intelligent.

## Resume Tailoring Approach

### The goal

The resume is not rewritten for every role. The bullets are fixed. What changes is emphasis and ordering.

### What to tailor

**The professional summary.** This is the only section that gets rewritten per application. It should reflect the language and priorities of the specific role, drawn from real experience.

**Which projects lead.** Order projects to surface the most relevant work first. If the role is infrastructure-heavy, Project M leads. If it is data engineering, GlobalFoundries or Project G leads.

**Which bullets stay.** Each project has more bullets than any single role needs. Cut bullets that have no relevance. Do not add bullets that are not true.

### What not to do

Do not change bullet content to match the job description language. If the experience does not exist, do not imply it does.

Do not remove honest gaps from the skills section. Closeable gaps stated honestly are better than gaps discovered in an interview.

### Honest gaps for this profile

- Cloud-native stack: Kubernetes, Terraform, AWS/GCP/Azure at production scale.
- Hands-on LLM and ML engineering.
- No hyperscale AI or GPU infrastructure.

These are closeable. State them honestly if directly asked. Do not volunteer them in the cover letter unless the role is specifically cloud-native and the gap is a blocker.

## Worked Example: Presto Drycleaners

**Role:** Staff Software Engineer, solo engineering role, operational systems focus.

**Gap read findings:**
- Driver routing listed as a feature to build. The real problem is upstream data quality, not the algorithm.
- Build vs buy listed as a responsibility. The unspoken risk is data lock-in when support lapses.
- Security not mentioned anywhere. A gap for a business handling payment data.
- Structured operational data will eventually enable demand forecasting and intelligent scheduling. They have not said this yet.

**Fit check:** Greenish. Solo role, implementation authority, blank canvas, operational problem space. In-person only is a yellow flag but not a dealbreaker given urgency.

**Cover letter approach used:**
- Led with identity, not credentials.
- Named three specific observations from the gap read, each tied to real experience.
- Planted the AI/data ceiling point without overselling it.
- Used carapex as a proof of independent systems thinking, not as a flex.
- Closed on fit, not on summary.

**What was avoided:**
- Mirroring their job description language.
- Framing build vs buy as "I won't buy solutions" (corrected to: I evaluate rigorously and own the data regardless).
- Framing vendor risk as ominous or legally inaccurate (corrected to: data becomes inaccessible, not "theirs").
- Naming the role a "blank canvas" when they did not say that.

## Checklist Before Sending

- [ ] Cover letter is under 500 words
- [ ] No dashes used
- [ ] No hedging connectors
- [ ] No mirrored job description language
- [ ] At least one "gap read" observation included
- [ ] One concrete proof point included
- [ ] Honest gaps not overclaimed
- [ ] Resume project order matches role priority
- [ ] Summary rewritten for this role
- [ ] Red lights resolved or explicitly accepted
